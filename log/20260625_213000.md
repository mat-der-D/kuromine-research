### 6. ABC conjecture analysis

**Formulation:** Divide $2^x + 3^y + 5 = z^3$ by 8: set z = 2w. Then:
$2^{x-3} + (3^y+5)/8 = w^3$, with A = 2^{x-3}, B = (3^y+5)/8 coprime (since v_2(B) = 0).

**ABC triple:** $(A, B, C) = (2^{x-3},\ (3^y+5)/8,\ w^3)$.
- rad(A) = 2
- rad(B) ≤ B ≈ 3^y/8
- rad(C) = rad(w) ≤ w ≈ 2^{x/3}/2

For the Theorem-5 family, y/x ≈ 1/2:
$$\frac{\log C}{\log \mathrm{rad}(ABC)} \approx \frac{x \log 2}{(\log_2 3 \cdot x/2 + x/3) \log 2}
= \frac{1}{0.7925 + 0.333} = \frac{1}{1.126} \approx 0.888 < 1.$$

**Conclusion:** The ABC quality is ≈ 0.888 < 1 for Kuromine. ABC asserts $C < \mathrm{rad}(ABC)^{1+\varepsilon}$, which is equivalent to quality < 1+ε. Since the actual quality is 0.888 < 1 < 1+ε for any ε>0, ABC is comfortably satisfied and gives no contradiction. ABC would only yield information if the quality exceeded 1, which it does not.

**Note:** This is consistent with the structural obstruction: the ABC conjecture is not violated because $3^y$ is a large term (comparable to $z^2$), making the sum "inefficient" as a cube but not in a way that violates ABC.

### 7. Literature survey: 2026 arXiv (new papers not in Sessions 6-7)

**arXiv:2505.19141** (Dong, Shafrir; published at STOC 2025):
"S-unit equations in modules and linear-exponential Diophantine equations."
Main result: decidability of whether a linear-exponential Diophantine equation over
two prime bases {p,q} (i.e., equations of the form Σ aᵢ · pˢⁱ · qᵗⁱ = 0 with
integer-valued unknowns sᵢ, tᵢ) has a solution. Based on Baker's theorem.

**Assessment for Kuromine:** NOT APPLICABLE. The Kuromine equation $2^x + 3^y + 5 = z^3$
has the term z^3 where z is an unknown algebraic integer NOT restricted to be an S-unit
in {2,3}. It cannot be recast as a linear-exponential equation over {2,3} because z can
have prime factors other than 2 and 3 (in fact, it does: z = 4 in the known solution
(5,3,4), and z > 2^{x/3} for Theorem-5). The decidability result requires ALL terms
to be of the form integer coefficient times product of integer powers of the two primes.

**arXiv:2604.18991** (Miyazaki, Scott, Styer; April 2026):
"Handling some Diophantine equation via Euclidean algorithm and its application to
purely exponential equations."
Main result: For fixed coprime positive integers a, b, c > 1, the equation
$a^x + b^y = c^z$ (Scott-Styer conjecture) has at most one positive-integer solution
in (x,y,z). Proved for c of the form 2^r·3+1 (for prime c ≡ 1 mod 3) and for c = 7, 13, 97.

**Assessment for Kuromine:** NOT APPLICABLE. Kuromine has z^3 with z as the variable
and 3 as the fixed exponent. The Miyazaki-Scott-Styer result addresses $c^z$ with c
fixed and z the exponent — a fundamentally different structure. Moreover, Kuromine has
an additive constant 5, absent from the Scott-Styer family.

**Summary:** No new applicable unconditional method found in 2026 literature.
