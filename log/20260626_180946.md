### C. The Eisenstein-norm density experiment (Wolfram — the new content)

For a genuine Theorem-5 solution the coprime factorization is $2^x+5 = A\cdot B$ with
$A = z - 3^j$, $B = z^2 + z\,3^j + 3^{2j}$, $w = 3^j$. Two long-recorded facts: $v_3(B)=0$
(so $3\nmid B$), and every prime factor of $B$ is $\equiv1\pmod3$ (so $B$ is a norm from
$\mathbb Z[\omega]$). The question never tested directly: **is "all prime factors of $B$ are
$\equiv1\pmod3$" an independent constraint** (which a random family member could violate, giving
a sieve), **or is it automatic?**

**Test 1 (verification on the known solution).** $(x,y,z)=(5,3,4)$: $A=1$, $B=4^2+4\cdot3+3^2=37$,
all prime factors of $37$ are $\equiv1\bmod3$, $2^5+5=1\cdot37$. ✓

**Test 2 (arbitrary $z\equiv1$, $w\equiv0\bmod3$, WITHOUT coprimality).** 8 random samples of
$B=z^2+zw+w^2$: the "all primes $\equiv1\bmod3$" condition **failed 6 of 8 times**. (When a prime
$p\equiv2\bmod3$ divides $\gcd(z,w)$ it enters $B$ to odd power, breaking the Eisenstein-norm
property.)

**Test 3 (same, WITH $\gcd(z,w)=1$ enforced).** 12 random samples: the condition held
**12 of 12 times**.

**Interpretation.** This reproduces a classical fact: the primitive values of the principal
Eisenstein form $z^2+zw+w^2$ (i.e. $\gcd(z,w)=1$) are exactly products of split primes
($p\equiv1\bmod3$) and possibly $3$. On the Theorem-5 family $z\equiv1\pmod3$ and $w=3^j$, so
$\gcd(z,w)=\gcd(z,3^j)=1$ **automatically**. Therefore the "all prime factors of $B$ are
$\equiv1\bmod3$" condition is **forced, not a coincidence to be sieved** — it is equivalent to
$\gcd(z,3^j)=1$, which the family satisfies by construction. The factorization gives no new
global constraint. This is the **multiplicative/density-side** version of the Session-4
soft-obstruction verdict (which argued it congruence-side: $A\equiv3$, $B\equiv37/3\pmod p$ for
all $p\ne3$, matching the phantom).
