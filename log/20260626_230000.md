### 2. Enumeration of all Thue-Mahler / norm-form castings — all broken by the constant 5

A Thue-Mahler equation has the form $|F(X,Y)| = (\text{S-unit})$ for an irreducible binary
form $F$ of degree $\geq 3$. I enumerated every way to cast Kuromine into this shape:

1. **$z^3 - 4W^3 = 3^y + 5$** with $W = 2^a$ (using $x=3a+2$, $2^x = 4W^3$; $S=\{2\}$).
   $F = z^3-4W^3$ is irreducible (4 not a cube), but the value $3^y+5$ is **not** a
   $\{2\}$-S-unit because of the $+5$. BROKEN.
2. **$z^3 - w^3 = 2^x + 5$** with $w = 3^j$ ($S=\{3\}$). RHS not a $\{3\}$-S-unit (the $+5$).
   BROKEN. (This is the Session-1 factorization $A\cdot B$.)
3. **Eisenstein norm form** $N_{\mathbb{Q}(\omega)}(z-w\omega) = B$, with $A\cdot B = 2^x+5$.
   Genuine norm form, but $A$ varies independently with $B$, so it is not a fixed-RHS
   Thue-Mahler equation; congruence content phantom-blocked (Session 4). BROKEN as a
   uniformity source.
4. **$N_{\mathbb{Q}(5^{1/3})}(z - 5^{1/3}) = z^3 - 5 = 2^x + 3^y$** (using that $x^3-5$ is
   irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$, verified Wolfram). Clean norm form on the left, but the RHS
   $2^x+3^y$ is a **sum of two S-units**, not one, with uncontrolled factorization. BROKEN.

**Root cause (all four):** the additive constant 5 (equivalently the two-term RHS) prevents
the RHS from being a single S-unit. This is exactly the Thue-Mahler face of the phantom
obstruction, and matches the Session-17 function-field finding that the constant is the hard
ingredient.

### 3. New symmetric reframing of the hard case (Wolfram-verified)

Combining $x=3a+2$ ($2^x=4(2^a)^3$, $W=2^a$) and $y=3j$ ($3^y=(3^j)^3$, $w=3^j$), the entire
Theorem-5 hard case is equivalent to the single identity
$$z^3 - 4\,(2^a)^3 - (3^j)^3 = 5.$$
Verified on the known solution $(5,3,4)$ ($a=1$, $j=1$): $4^3 - 4\cdot2^3 - 3^3 = 64-32-27 = 5$.
This is "a cube minus four times an S-unit-cube minus an S-unit-cube equals 5" — the most
symmetric statement of the hard case found so far, making transparent that the obstruction is
the constant 5 on the right, not the cube structure. (It does not apply to $(1,0,2)$, whose
$x=1\not\equiv2\pmod3$; that solution is outside the Theorem-5 progression, as expected.)

### 4. The forced prime factor 37 of B (new clean fact) and its soft-obstructed branches

I computed, for all primes $p\equiv1\pmod3$ up to ~280, which divide $2^x+5$ over the
Theorem-5 family (Wolfram). For most such $p$ the only $x$-class is $x\equiv5$ and
$p\nmid2^x+5$. The decisive exception:

> **$37 \mid 2^x+5$ for every Theorem-5 solution, with $v_{37}(2^x+5)=1$ exactly**
> (Wolfram: constant $=1$ over $k_x=0..8$; because $x\equiv5\pmod{36}=\mathrm{ord}_{37}(2)$,
> so $2^x\equiv2^5=32\equiv-5\pmod{37}$). Hence $37\|B$ (since $37\equiv1\pmod3$ can only
> divide $B$, and $\gcd(A,B)=1$).

Since $37=\pi\bar\pi$ splits in $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ ($37 = 3^2-3\cdot7+7^2$), and the mod-37
cube equation $z^3\equiv 11\pmod{37}$ has three roots $z\in\{21,25,28\}$ (Wolfram;
$w=3^j\equiv25$, $z^3\equiv 2^x+3^y+5\equiv 32+11+5\equiv11$), there are three branches:

| $z \bmod 37$ | $z/w \bmod 37$ | where 37 goes | $A=z-w \bmod 37$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 28 | 10 (cube root of 1) | $37\mid B$, $\pi$ branch | 3  — **phantom branch** |
| 21 | 26 (cube root of 1) | $37\mid B$, $\bar\pi$ branch | 33 |
| 25 | 1                    | $37\mid A$              | 0  |

The phantom $(10/3,1/3)$ reduces to $z\equiv28$, $A\equiv3\pmod{37}$ — the $\pi$ branch.

**Soft obstruction confirmed at all 37-adic depths.** A real solution could a priori be on
any of the three branches; mod 37 cannot decide. Mod $37^n$ cannot decide either: the phantom
is a global rational cube, hence a cube mod $37^n$ for all $n$ (verified mod $37^3$, Wolfram:
$(10/3)^3 \equiv 48814 \equiv 2^5+3^{-3}+5 \pmod{37^3}$). At depth $37^2$ the residues
$2^x,3^y\pmod{37^2}$ already vary across the family ($332640\not\equiv0\bmod
\mathrm{ord}_{37^2}(2)=1332$; $55440\not\equiv0\bmod\mathrm{ord}_{37^2}(3)=666$), so there is
no universal deeper 37-adic constraint. No contradiction.

### 5. Per-fixed-$y$ Thue equation: finiteness without uniformity

For fixed $y$, $z^3-4W^3=3^y+5$ is a genuine Thue equation. Searching $W\leq1000$ (Wolfram)
over $y=0..12$:
- $y=3$: $(z,W)=(4,2)$, and $W=2=2^1$ is a power of 2 $\Rightarrow$ known solution $(5,3,4)$.
- $y=5$: $(z,W)=(54,34)$, but $34$ is not a power of 2 $\Rightarrow$ no Kuromine solution.
- all other $y\leq12$: no positive $(z,W)$ Thue solution.

So per fixed $y$ the equation is finite and the residual constraint is "$W=2^a$." Thue-Mahler
theory (Evertse-Győry quantitative bounds) bounds the **number** of solutions per equation in
terms of the unit rank of $\mathbb{Q}(2^{1/3})$ — which is $1$ (signature $(1,1)$; fundamental
unit $2^{1/3}-1$, norm $1$, Wolfram) — and $|S|$. This gives a finite bound **per $y$**, never
a single contradiction for the whole infinite $y$-progression. Finiteness per fixed exponent
is already known; uniformity over the progression is the open content, and Thue-Mahler theory
does not deliver it.
