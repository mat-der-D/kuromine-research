### 1. Literature survey: 2025-2026 arXiv

Searched for new methods applicable to equations with two comparable large exponential terms summing to a perfect cube. Found four relevant papers:

- **arXiv:2510.11753** (Cai, Oct 2025): "An Effective Method for Solving a Class of Transcendental Diophantine Equations." Handles a^x + b = c^y — ONE exponential unknown. Algorithm computes a strict upper bound then enumerates. Not applicable to Kuromine's TWO exponential unknowns (2^x + 3^y + 5 = z^3).

- **arXiv:2601.11376** (Müller–Taktikos, Jan 2026): "From ABC to Effective Roth and Ridout Constants for Cubic Roots." Derives effective Roth/Ridout constants for cube roots assuming the ABC conjecture. Conditional on ABC; and even if applied unconditionally, would face the κ_real ≈ 0.62 obstruction documented in Session 3 (the two-S-units regime is structurally beyond single cubic irrationality measures).

- **arXiv:2602.19061** (Müller, Feb 2026): "Gain Bounds for Diagonal Superelliptic Equations under the Strong ABC Conjecture." Studies x^n + k = y^m with ABC. Conditional and structurally mismatched (Kuromine has two exponential terms, not a single power on the left).

- **arXiv:2506.20909** (Jun 2025): "Diophantine Equations over Z." Reduces exponentially Diophantine equations to ordinary ones using Lucas sequences as a standard method. The Kuromine equation 2^x + 3^y + 5 = z^3 does not fit the required form (the additive constant 5 and the specific shape prevent a Lucas-sequence reduction).

**Conclusion:** No new unconditional method for the two-comparable-S-units regime found in 2025-2026 literature. The fundamental gap (κ_real ≈ 0.62 vs any μ ≥ 2) documented in Session 3 remains open.

### 2. Descent-on-A investigation

The central question: can we show A = z - 3^j must divide something small, forcing A = 1?

**Step 1: Prime factorization structure of A.** Proved (reconfirming and strengthening Session 4):

*Theorem.* Every prime p ≡ 2 (mod 3) dividing 2^x + 5 must divide A.

*Proof.* If p | B = z^2 + z·3^j + (3^j)^2, then taking 3^j as a unit mod p (gcd(3^j, p) = 1 for p ≠ 3), we get (z/3^j)^2 + (z/3^j) + 1 ≡ 0 (mod p). This polynomial has solutions iff its discriminant -3 is a quadratic residue mod p. But -3 is a QR mod p iff p ≡ 1 (mod 3). For p ≡ 2 (mod 3), -3 is NOT a QR, so p ∤ B, hence p | A. □

**Step 2: Computational check.** For k = 1, 3 in the Theorem-5 family: found primes ≡ 2 (mod 3) dividing 2^x + 5 (e.g., 9491 for k=1, 59 for k=3). These are real prime factors of A, confirming A is large. For k=2,4,5 no such prime ≤ 100,000 was found (these appear at larger primes). This is consistent with A ~ 2^(x/3) having large prime factors ≡ 2 (mod 3), but those factors can be huge.

**Step 3: Descent mechanism absent.** No mechanism found to force A | something small. The equation A · B = 2^x + 5 with A ~ 2^(x/3), B ~ 2^(2x/3) offers no natural descent because both A and B grow with x at the same rate as the equation's size. There is no "smaller instance" to reduce to.

### 3. New refined constraints on A and j

Let x = 5 + 332640k, y = 166317 + 166320k (k ≥ 1), j = y/3 = 55439 + 55440k.

**Theorem A: j ≡ 7 (mod 8).**

*Proof.* j = 55439 + 55440k. 55439 mod 8 = 7 (since 55439 = 6929·8 + 7). 55440 mod 8 = 0 (since 55440 = 6930·8). So j ≡ 7 (mod 8) for all k ≥ 1. □

**Theorem B: A ≡ 27 (mod 32) for any Theorem-5 solution.**

*Proof.* From Session 2: z ≡ 6 (mod 32). From Theorem A: j ≡ 7 (mod 8). ord_32(3) = 8, and 3^7 ≡ 2187 ≡ 11 (mod 32). So 3^j ≡ 11 (mod 32) for all k.

B = z^2 + z·3^j + (3^j)^2 ≡ 36 + 6·11 + 121 = 36 + 66 + 121 = 223 ≡ 31 (mod 32).

For x ≥ 5: 2^x ≡ 0 (mod 32), so 2^x + 5 ≡ 5 (mod 32).

Since A · B ≡ 5 (mod 32) and gcd(B, 32) = gcd(31, 32) = 1: A ≡ 5 · 31^{-1} (mod 32). Since 31 ≡ -1 (mod 32), 31^{-1} ≡ -1 ≡ 31 (mod 32). So A ≡ 5 · 31 = 155 ≡ 155 - 4·32 = 155 - 128 = 27 (mod 32). □

**Theorem C: A ≡ 4 (mod 9) for any Theorem-5 solution.**

*Proof.* Since y ≥ 3 for all Theorem-5 solutions, 3^y ≡ 0 (mod 27). The Theorem-5 family has x ≡ 5 (mod ord_27(2)) — verified: ord_27(2) = 18, and 332640 mod 18 = 0, so 2^x ≡ 2^5 = 32 ≡ 5 (mod 27). Therefore z^3 ≡ 2^x + 5 ≡ 10 (mod 27). The cube roots of 10 mod 27 are {4, 13, 22}, all of which satisfy r ≡ 4 (mod 9). Since j ≥ 2 for all Theorem-5 solutions, 3^j ≡ 0 (mod 9), so A = z - 3^j ≡ z (mod 9) ≡ 4 (mod 9). □

*Remark.* Previously only A ≡ {1, 4, 7} (mod 9) was known. This pins A ≡ 4 (mod 9) precisely.

**Theorem D: A ≡ 22 (mod 27) for any Theorem-5 solution.**

*Proof.* z^3 ≡ 37 (mod 81) [computed: 2^x + 5 ≡ 37 mod 81 for all Theorem-5 k]. Cube roots of 37 mod 81 are {22, 49, 76}. All three satisfy r ≡ 22 (mod 27) (since 49 mod 27 = 22, 76 mod 27 = 22). Since 3^j ≡ 0 (mod 27) for large j, A ≡ z ≡ 22 (mod 27). □

**Combined constraint via CRT.** A ≡ 22 (mod 27) and A ≡ 27 (mod 32) combine (lcm = 864) to A ≡ 859 (mod 864). Refining to mod 81 and mod 32 (lcm = 2592): three branches

$$A \equiv \{1723, 859, 2587\} \pmod{2592}$$

corresponding to z ≡ {22, 49, 76} mod 81 respectively. All three satisfy A ≡ 4 mod 9, A ≡ 22 mod 27, A ≡ 27 mod 32.

### 4. 3-adic lifting analysis

Computed the Hensel lifting of cube roots of 2^x + 5 modulo 3^n:

| n | 3^n | 2^x+5 mod 3^n | cube roots mod 3^n |
|---|-----|---------------|---------------------|
| 1 | 3 | 1 | {1} |
| 2 | 9 | 1 | {1, 4, 7} |
| 3 | 27 | 10 | {4, 13, 22} |
| 4 | 81 | 37 | {22, 49, 76} |
| 5 | 243 | 199 | {22, 103, 184} |
| 6 | 729 | 442 | {22, 265, 508} |
| 7 | 2187 | 1900 | {22, 751, 1480} |

Key pattern: for n ≥ 4, the three cube roots of 2^x + 5 mod 3^n are r, r + 3^{n-1}, r + 2·3^{n-1} where r is the unique root mod 3^{n-1}. Since 2^x + 5 ≡ 37 (mod 81) for all Theorem-5 k (because 2^x ≡ 2^5 = 32 mod 81), the Hensel-lifted branches all converge to 37's cube root system in Z_3.

**The 3^n wall re-expressed:** 37 being a cube mod 3^n for all n means z can exist as a 3-adic integer, so no 3-adic constraint can refute the existence of z. This is consistent with what Sessions 2 and 4 established and is not circumventable.
