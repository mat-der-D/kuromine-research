### 2. Affine Chabauty I (Leonhardt–Lüdtke, arXiv:2511.15949, Nov 2025)

**Paper content (from web search, abstract + comments):**

Proves finiteness of S-integral points on affine curves satisfying a rank-genus inequality
for the generalized Jacobian, by embedding the affine curve into its generalized Jacobian
and bounding the Abel-Jacobi image of S-integral points using arithmetic intersection theory.
The rank-genus condition generalizes the classical Chabauty condition (rank of Jacobian
< genus) to the affine setting (rank < genus + number of cusps + |S| - 1 or similar).

**Assessment:**

The method applies to algebraic curves $C/\mathbb{Q}$ with coordinates $(P, Q) \in
\mathbb{A}^n(\mathbb{Q})$, seeking points where all coordinates are S-integers.

Kuromine cannot be directly cast in this framework. The variables $x$ and $y$ are
*discrete exponential variables* (they appear as exponents of 2 and 3), not algebraic
coordinates on a curve. The only way to produce a curve is:

- *Fix x:* For fixed $x$, the equation $z^3 = 3^y + (2^x + 5)$ asks when an integer of
  the form $3^y + C$ is a perfect cube. Taking $w = 3^y$ gives the algebraic relation
  $z^3 - w = C$, an affine curve in $(z, w)$ — but the constraint "$w$ is a power of 3"
  is not an S-integrality condition. S-integrality on the affine line with $S = \{3\}$
  means $w \in \mathbb{Z}[1/3]$, which is far broader than $\{1, 3, 9, 27, \ldots\}$.

- *Fix y:* Similarly, fixing $y$ and seeking $z^3 = 2^x + C$ gives the algebraic
  relation $z^3 = u + C$ with $u = 2^x$ — again $u$ being a power of 2 is not an
  S-integrality condition on the curve $z^3 - u = C$.

**Structural mismatch (precise diagnosis):**

The Chabauty framework handles S-integral points on algebraic curves; S-integrality
records which denominators are allowed (i.e., membership in $\prod_{p \in S} p^{\mathbb{Z}}$
as a multiplicative subgroup). The Kuromine constraint "$2^x + 3^y + 5 = z^3$ for
non-negative integers $x, y$" is NOT equivalent to any S-integrality condition on an
algebraic curve, because:

1. The set $\{2^x : x \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}\} = \{1, 2, 4, 8, \ldots\}$ is a discrete
   sub-semigroup of $\mathbb{Z}$, not the set of S-integers in any algebraic curve.
2. The constraint "$u = 2^x$ for integer $x$" on the curve $z^3 - u - 3^y = 5$
   is a Diophantine condition external to the algebraic geometry of the curve.

**Status: NOT APPLICABLE (structural).**

### 3. Affine Chabauty II (Leonhardt–Lüdtke, arXiv:2602.05643, Feb 2026)

**Paper content:** Provides the algorithm for computing the S-integral points whose
finiteness is established in Affine Chabauty I. Method: constructs explicit logarithmic
differentials whose Coleman integrals vanish on S-integral points; uses p-adic residue
theorem for Coleman integrals of log differentials.

**Assessment:** Shares the same structural inapplicability as Affine Chabauty I.
Even if one could set up the right affine curve, the algorithm would return all
S-integral points on that algebraic curve — which is a strictly larger set than those
with $w = 3^y$ for integer $y$. The extra Diophantine condition on the exponents cannot
be encoded in the Coleman-function vanishing condition.

**Status: NOT APPLICABLE (structural, same reason as I).**

### 4. Modular Chabauty (Zehavi, arXiv:2505.12947, May 2025)

**Paper content (from web search):**

Presents an unconditional algorithm for S-integral points on any *elliptic moduli problem*
— a geometrically connected curve $\mathcal{Y}$ carrying a non-isotrivial elliptic
fibration $\pi: \mathcal{E} \to \mathcal{Y}$. The modular period map $\Phi_\mathcal{M}:
\mathcal{Y} \to \mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ plays the role of the p-adic period map in
Chabauty-type arguments. The algorithm proceeds in two steps: (a) enumerate all rational
elliptic curves with good reduction outside $S$ (Effective Shafarevich); (b) compute the
S-integral points in the fibre of $\Phi_\mathcal{M}$. Applies to cubic Thue/Thue-Mahler,
Mordell, Ramanujan-Nagell, and the thrice-punctured line.

**Assessment:**

The applicability condition is that the problem can be phrased as: find S-integral points
on a curve $\mathcal{Y}$ carrying an elliptic fibration. The natural candidates for
$\mathcal{Y}$ from Kuromine are:

- *Mordell-style view:* For fixed $x = x_0$, the curve $\mathcal{Y}_{x_0}: w^3 - z^3 =
  -(2^{x_0}+5)$ (i.e., $z^3 - w^3 = N$ with $N = 2^{x_0}+5$ fixed). This IS a Mordell-
  type (genus 1) curve, and the Modular Chabauty method can in principle find all its
  integral points. But one needs additionally that $w = 3^j$ for integer $j$ — which
  is an external condition on the integral points, not handled by the framework.

- *Family view:* The Theorem-5 family has $x = 5 + 332640 k_x$ for $k_x \geq 1$, so $N$
  varies with $k_x$. Each member of the family gives a different Mordell curve; there is
  no single algebraic curve encoding the whole family.

**Partial applicability:** For each *fixed* $k_x$ (hence fixed $N_{k_x} = 2^{5+332640k_x}+5$),
Zehavi's algorithm could in principle verify that the Thue equation $z^3 - w^3 = N_{k_x}$
has no solution with $w$ a power of 3. But:

(a) $N_{k_x}$ is an astronomically large number (log $N_{k_x} \approx 230572$ for $k_x = 1$),
    far beyond the practical range of the Effective Shafarevich step (which enumerates
    elliptic curves by conductor — here conductor ≈ $N_{k_x}^2 \approx e^{461144}$, which
    Cremona's database does not cover and never will for $k_x \geq 1$).

(b) Even for $k_x = 0$ (the known solution at $x = 5$, $y = 3$, $z = 4$), $N_0 = 37$
    and the method would just rediscover the known solution.

(c) The method gives finiteness + enumeration *per* fixed $k_x$, but gives no information
    about the infinite family $k_x \geq 1$.

**Status: NOT APPLICABLE (structural, for the same reason as all fixed-$k_x$ approaches;
the $k_x \to \infty$ problem is the entire difficulty).**

### 5. Cubic Chabauty (Balakrishnan–Bianchi–Dogra, arXiv:2604.20662, Apr 2026)

**Paper content (from web search):**

Extends Kim's method to depth 3 for an elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ of Mordell-Weil rank
at most 2, giving the Kim set in depth 3 as the zeroes of a polynomial in p-adic elliptic
polylogarithms (under an assumption on non-vanishing of a special value of a p-adic
L-function). Verifies new instances of Kim's conjecture.

**Assessment:**

This method operates on a fixed elliptic curve $E$ and finds $S$-integral (or $S$-unit)
points on $E$ or its covering curves. It requires a fixed, explicit elliptic curve as
input. The Kuromine problem does not naturally produce a single fixed elliptic curve over
which all solutions live. (Fixing $x$ or $y$ yields a Mordell curve that varies with the
parameter.) The depth-3 p-adic polylogarithm construction is designed for the fixed-curve
case and does not encode the family of curves arising from the Theorem-5 progression.

Moreover, even if one applied Cubic Chabauty to a Mordell curve $z^3 - w^3 = N$ for a
specific $N$, the output would be the $S$-integral points on that curve — not specifically
those with $w = 3^j$. The extra arithmetic constraint on $w$ is external.

**Status: NOT APPLICABLE (structural).**

### 6. Mathematical analysis: What the Chabauty-Kim barrier means for Kuromine

The common thread across all four papers is:

**Algebraic geometry / Chabauty-Kim methods operate on algebraic curves and their
S-integral (or rational) points. They are ideal when the Diophantine problem can be
rephrased as: "find the S-integral points on a specific algebraic curve C." The Kuromine
problem cannot be so rephrased in a useful way, because:**

1. The variables $x$ and $y$ appear as exponents, not coordinates.
2. Taking $u = 2^x$, $v = 3^y$ introduces coordinates $u, v$ living on the "curve"
   $\{(u, v) : u = 2^x,\, v = 3^y,\, x, y \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}\}$, which is a
   **discrete** (hence non-algebraic) subset of $\mathbb{A}^2$.
3. Any algebraic curve through $(2^x, 3^y)$ for all $x, y$ would be the entire affine
   plane (no non-trivial polynomial vanishes on this discrete set in a constraining way).

This is the **exponential barrier for Chabauty-Kim**: all Chabauty-type methods (classical,
affine, Kim, quadratic, cubic, modular) are fundamentally about *algebraic* geometry and
its p-adic avatars. The exponential structure of Kuromine (two independently varying
exponential terms) lies outside the algebraic category, regardless of the depth of the
Kim tower used.

The only scenario where Chabauty-Kim would apply to Kuromine is if one could *fix* both
$x$ and $y$ and look at the resulting trivial equation $z^3 = C$ for a constant $C$ — but
this is trivial and not useful. Fixing one of $x$ or $y$ and varying the other still leaves
an exponential variable.

**Formal statement of the barrier:**
> The Kuromine equation $2^x + 3^y + 5 = z^3$ is an *exponential Diophantine equation*
> in the sense that $x$ and $y$ appear as exponents of fixed bases. The solution set does
> not lie on any algebraic variety over $\mathbb{Q}$; it is an arithmetic (exponential)
> set. Chabauty-Kim methods apply to algebraic varieties. No Chabauty-Kim method can
> directly apply to an exponential Diophantine equation.
>
> This is **not** a failure of existing implementations or a quantitative gap — it is a
> categorical mismatch. Improving the depth of the Kim tower, the rank condition, or the
> computational reach of Modular Chabauty cannot change this.

## Conclusion

Session 13 systematically closes the Chabauty-Kim / algebraic-geometry cluster of methods —
including its newest and most powerful 2025–2026 instantiations. The non-applicability is
categorical, not quantitative: exponential Diophantine equations in which two variables
appear as exponents (the "two-exponential" structure of Kuromine) lie outside the algebraic
category that all Chabauty-type methods require.

The research has now diagnosed **five independent categorical barriers**, each ruling out
a major branch of modern Diophantine techniques:

1. **Congruence sieving** (Sessions 1–2): phantom solution is locally solvable everywhere.
2. **Baker-type methods** (Sessions 1, 5): $v_p(\Lambda_p) = 0$ at both $p = 2$ and $p = 3$;
   no linear form is small in any metric (two-S-units regime).
3. **Modular method / Frey curves** (Session 4): no varying prime exponent; fixed small
   cube exponent.
4. **Chabauty-Kim methods (all forms)** (Session 13): categorical mismatch — exponential
   variables $x, y$ as exponents of fixed bases lie outside the algebraic category.
5. **Effective abc / quality-based bounds** (Sessions 8, 12): Kuromine's triple quality
   ≈ 0.888 < 1, so upper-bounding quality is vacuous.

The open target remains unchanged: **an unconditional effective lower bound for |z^3 - 3^y|
with exponent κ < 1.107 in the two-comparable-S-units regime.** No such tool exists in
the 2025–2026 literature, and the Chabauty-Kim cluster (the last major unexplored
algebraic-geometry branch) does not supply it.

The Kuromine problem is genuinely open at the research frontier.
